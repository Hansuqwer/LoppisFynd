# Behance Scan - Color Palettes (2024-2026)

Source checked:
- Behance search: https://www.behance.net/search/projects/color%20palettes

## What Shows Up Repeatedly

1. Palettes are presented as moodboards, not just swatches
- Palette chips next to photography, paper textures, materials (wood/ceramic/plant), and brand patterns.
- Takeaway for the app: our palette has to look good on top of textures + atmospheric images, not only on flat fills.

2. Warm neutrals + earthy accents are dominant in "hospitality" and craft brands
- Terracotta, sand, warm ivory, olive/sage, and a deep ink show up constantly.
- This aligns extremely well with "Nature Distilled".

3. Designers still include a true dark anchor
- Even when everything is warm and soft, there is almost always a deep ink for typography and contrast.

4. Gradients remain popular, but mostly as a brand asset
- Gradient guides and palette collections exist, but they are typically used for hero visuals, posters, social, and "moments".
- For LoppisFynd: keep gradients as optional accents (badges, onboarding hero), not as default surfaces.

## What To Steal For LoppisFynd

- Always pair the palette with materials (paper/linen) and one atmospheric photo background in docs and design comps.
- Keep the palette small in UI (neutrals + ink + terracotta + one cool counterweight), and put variety into textures.
- Make ink explicit: treat it as a first-class ramp (not an afterthought).

## Token Implications

- Ramps matter more than named swatches for product UI:
  - neutral ramp (background -> surface -> border)
  - ink ramp (textPrimary -> muted)
  - terracotta ramp (CTA states)
  - cool ramp (info/focus)
- Use swatch names for brand story; use ramps for implementation.
